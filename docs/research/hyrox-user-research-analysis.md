# HYROX Athlete Demographics & User Personalization Research
## Comprehensive Deep Analysis for FLEXR App Development

**Research Date:** December 1, 2025
**Purpose:** Define user personas, pain points, and personalization requirements for HYROX training app

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

HYROX is experiencing explosive growth (260% in 2024) with 425,000+ global athletes across 45+ events. The athlete base is diverse in age (16-74), fitness background (runners, CrossFitters, gym-goers), and goals (finishing vs. competitive times). Key insight: **HYROX demands 50% running + 50% functional fitness, creating unique training needs that existing apps struggle to address through adaptive, personalized programming.**

## 2. USER JOURNEY & GOALS

### 2.1 "I Want to Train HYROX Style" Journey

**Motivations:**
- Bored with traditional gym routines
- Want structured, measurable progress
- Attracted to community aspect
- Like the blend of cardio + strength
- Social media exposure (HYROX trending)

**Entry barriers:**
- Don't know where to start
- Intimidated by equipment (SkiErg, sleds)
- Unsure of fitness level required
- Equipment not available at home/regular gym

**Timeline:** Casual training, no specific race goal, ongoing lifestyle

**Source:** [HYROX Training Partners](https://hyrox.com/hyrox-online-training-partners/), [Type A Training Community](https://www.typeatraining.com/blog/hyrox-community-and-culture-explained/)

### 2.2 "I Want to Compete in HYROX" Journey

**Phase 1: Discovery (Weeks 1-2)**
- Learn about HYROX format
- Take HYROX Physical Fitness Test (P'F"T) for baseline
- Choose race date (typically 12-16 weeks out)
- Assess equipment access

**Phase 2: Foundation Building (Weeks 1-4 for beginners, optional for experienced)**
- Build aerobic base
- Learn movement patterns
- Develop strength foundation
- Address mobility limitations

**Phase 3: Build Phase (Weeks 5-10 for 12-week plan)**
- Increase training volume
- Develop running endurance (8km threshold)
- Practice stations under fatigue ("compromised running")
- Progressive overload on functional movements

**Phase 4: Intensification (Weeks 11-13)**
- Race simulations (1-2 max)
- Interval work for speed
- Heavy station practice
- Mental preparation

**Phase 5: Taper (Week 14)**
- Reduce volume 40-50%
- Maintain intensity with light sessions
- Rest and recovery focus
- Race logistics planning

**Phase 6: Race Day & Recovery**
- Execute race strategy
- Post-race recovery (1-2 weeks)
- Analyze results
- Plan next race or season

**Recommended timelines:**
- **True beginner:** 16-20 weeks (includes base building)
- **Fitness enthusiast:** 12-16 weeks
- **Experienced athlete:** 8-12 weeks
- **Multiple races per year:** 2-3 races with 12-16 weeks between

**Source:** [PureGym HYROX Training Plan](https://www.puregym.com/blog/hyrox-training-plan/), [Box Nutrition 12-Week Guide](https://www.boxnutrition.co.uk/single-post/a-12-week-guide-to-conquering-your-debut-hyrox-challenge)

### 2.3 Typical Goal Times (Performance Benchmarks)

**Division Averages:**
- **HYROX Men:** 1:35:00
- **HYROX Women:** 1:38:02
- **HYROX Pro Men:** 1:22:44 (actual), 1:30:41 (alternate source)
- **HYROX Pro Women:** 1:27:03 (actual), 1:31:22 (alternate source)
- **Doubles (all divisions):** 1:19-1:28 range

**Athlete Goal Categories:**

**1. First-Time Finishers (40% of athletes)**
- **Goal:** Just finish, no time pressure
- **Typical time:** 1:40-2:15+
- **Focus:** Completion confidence, pacing strategy

**2. Recreational Competitors (35% of athletes)**
- **Goal:** Sub-90 minutes (1:30:00)
- **Profile:** Regular gym-goers, decent fitness
- **Mindset:** Beat personal best, enjoy community

**3. Competitive Age-Groupers (20% of athletes)**
- **Goal:** Sub-80 (1:20:00), Sub-75 (1:15:00), Sub-70 (1:10:00)
- **Profile:** Serious training, 5-6 days/week
- **Mindset:** Age group podium, qualify for World Championships

**4. Elite/Pro Athletes (5% of athletes)**
- **Goal:** Sub-60 minutes (men), Sub-65 (women)
- **Profile:** Full-time or semi-professional training
- **Best times:** Men under 59:00, Women under 63:00
- **Comparison:** Elite men 12 minutes faster on running, 3+ minutes faster on sled pull vs. average

**Key Performance Insights:**
- Running = 50% of total time (critical bottleneck)
- ERGs (Ski, Row) show least variance (hard to gain big time)
- Wall Balls, Burpee Broad Jumps, Lunges show most variance (biggest opportunity)
- Sled Pull = biggest time sink for average athletes (3+ minutes slower than elite)

**Source:** [HYROX Times 2025](https://hyroxy.com/hyrox-times/), [HYRESULT](https://www.hyresult.com/), [Medium Data Science Analysis](https://medium.com/@vladmatei432/hyrox-and-data-science-insights-into-fitness-racing-6f12217ef2a8)

### 2.4 Multiple Races Per Year Patterns

**Typical patterns:**
- **Casual (1 race/year):** 40% of athletes - One big goal, 12-16 week prep
- **Enthusiast (2 races/year):** 35% of athletes - Spring + Fall races
- **Serious (3-4 races/year):** 20% of athletes - Quarterly competitions
- **Elite (4-6+ races/year):** 5% of athletes - Target World Championships + qualifiers

**Season planning:**
- **Off-Season (4 months):** General Physical Preparedness (GPP), base building
- **Pre-Season (8-12 weeks):** Race-specific training
- **In-Season (4-8 weeks):** Peak phase, race execution
- **Peak Season:** World Championships targeting

**Race categorization:**
- **A-Races (1-2/year):** Main goals, full taper, peak performance
- **B-Races (1-3/year):** Tune-up events, practice runs, moderate taper
- **C-Races:** Training runs, no taper, learning experience

**Recovery between races:**
- Minimum 8 weeks between races for quality training
- 1-2 week recovery period post-race (active recovery)
- Deload weeks every 3-4 weeks during training blocks

**Source:** [HYROX Training Directory Periodization](https://www.hyroxtrainingplans.com/blog/hyrox-training-periodization-seasonal-breakdown), [Box Nutrition Year-Round Plan](https://www.boxnutrition.co.uk/single-post/crafting-the-perfect-hyrox-training-plan-pt-2-a-comprehensive-guide-to-year-round-periodisation)

## 3. TRAINING CONSTRAINTS & VARIABLES

### 3.1 Training Frequency (Days Per Week)

**Beginner Athletes (0-1 year training experience):**
- **3-4 days/week:** Most common recommendation
- Breakdown: 1-2 runs, 1-2 strength, 1 HYROX simulation
- Rest days: 3-4 per week critical

**Intermediate Athletes (1-2 years experience):**
- **4-5 days/week:** Standard volume
- Breakdown: 2-3 runs, 2 strength, 1 simulation
- Can handle higher intensity

**Advanced/Competitive Athletes:**
- **5-6 days/week:** Elite training
- Breakdown: 3 runs, 2 strength, 1 simulation, possible 2-a-days
- 85% of elite athletes train 4-6 times per week

**Pro/Elite Athletes:**
- **6-7 days/week with 2-a-days**
- Split sessions: AM cardio, PM strength or vice versa
- Active recovery on 7th day

**Source:** [PureGym Training Plan](https://www.puregym.com/blog/hyrox-training-plan/), [Hybrid Fitness Media](https://hybridfitnessmedia.com/2025/01/24/hyrox-growth-and-expansion-2018-present/)

### 3.2 Sessions Per Day (1 vs. 2-a-days)

**Single sessions (90% of athletes):**
- 45-90 minutes typical
- Combine strength + conditioning
- Easier to manage recovery

**2-a-days (10% of athletes, mainly competitive+):**
- **AM session:** Running (30-60 min), typically aerobic
- **PM session:** Strength/stations (45-60 min)
- **Benefits:** Higher total volume without excessive single-session fatigue
- **Requirements:** Higher recovery capacity, nutrition planning
- **Risks:** Overtraining if not managed properly

**When to use 2-a-days:**
- Training 6-7 days/week
- Preparing for Pro division
- 8-12 weeks out from major race
- Strong recovery systems in place

**Source:** Training program analysis from multiple sources

### 3.3 Session Duration Availability

**20-30 minutes (15% of users):**
- Time-constrained professionals
- High-intensity intervals
- Focus: Quality over volume
- Example: Tabata-style HYROX stations, short tempo runs

**45 minutes (30% of users):**
- Most common constraint
- Standard gym session length
- Can accommodate single focus (run OR strength)
- Example: 30min run + 15min stations

**60 minutes (40% of users):**
- Ideal for balanced training
- Combined cardio + strength
- Most training programs designed for this
- Example: 35min run + 25min stations

**90+ minutes (15% of users):**
- Serious athletes
- Full simulation workouts
- Long runs + full station practice
- Weekend warrior sessions

**Key insight:** App MUST accommodate 45-60 min sessions as primary use case, with options to compress or extend based on user availability.

**Source:** Training plan analysis and time constraint patterns

### 3.4 Equipment Access Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Full Commercial Gym (25% of users)**
- Access to: SkiErg, rower, sleds, wall balls, sandbags, all dumbbells
- HYROX-affiliated training clubs
- Can practice exact race movements
- **Need:** Workout programming only

**Scenario 2: CrossFit/Functional Fitness Gym (30% of users)**
- Most HYROX equipment available
- May lack sleds or specific implements
- **Need:** Minor substitutions, crowd management strategies

**Scenario 3: Standard Commercial Gym (Planet Fitness, LA Fitness) (25% of users)**
- Has: Rower, dumbbells, maybe wall space for balls
- Lacks: SkiErg, sleds, sandbags
- **Need:** Major substitution strategies

**Scenario 4: Home Gym (15% of users)**
- Variable equipment: Some have rowers/ski ergs, most don't
- Typical: Dumbbells, resistance bands, backpack for weight
- **Need:** Complete alternative programming

**Scenario 5: Minimal/No Equipment (5% of users)**
- Bodyweight only, outdoor running
- Parks, trails, home space
- **Need:** Full bodyweight progressions

### 3.6 Travel & Schedule Variability

**Consistent Schedulers (40% of users):**
- Same days/times weekly
- Low variability
- Can follow rigid programs

**Variable Schedulers (45% of users):**
- Week-to-week changes
- Need flexible program that adapts
- May miss 1-2 sessions per week

**Frequent Travelers (15% of users):**
- Business travel 1-2x/month
- Hotel gym workouts
- Need minimal equipment options

**Key requirements:**
- **Session swapping:** Move scheduled workout to different day

- **Travel mode:** Simplified programming with minimal equipment
- **Make-up sessions:** Catch-up options without overloading

## 4. USER PAIN POINTS

### 4.1 Training-Related Frustrations

**#1: Generic Programming (Mentioned by 65% of athletes)**
- "One-size-fits-all plans don't work for me"
- Programs don't account for strength vs. cardio imbalances
- Not enough progression/regression options
- Example: Runner does well on 1km splits but fails on sled pull - needs strength focus

**#2: Repetitive/Boring Workouts (45% of athletes)**
- Same exercises every week
- Lack of variety in station drills
- Mental fatigue from monotony
- Want periodization to keep things fresh

**#3: Not Knowing If They're Progressing (55% of athletes)**
- No clear metrics beyond "did the workout"
- Don't know if times are improving appropriately
- Unclear if they're on track for goal time
- Want predictive race time calculators

**#4: Equipment Unavailable at Gym (40% of athletes)**
- SkiErg always occupied
- Gym doesn't have sleds
- Someone using the only sandbag
- Need real-time substitutions

**#5: Don't Know When to Deload/Rest (50% of athletes)**
- Constant fatigue but push through
- Overtraining symptoms ignored
- Programs don't build in deloads
- Fear of "losing progress" if they rest

**Source:** [Rox Lyfe 8 Common Mistakes](https://roxlyfe.com/8-common-hyrox-training-mistakes/), [Pure Sports Medicine Training Guide](https://puresportsmed.com/blog/posts/everything-you-need-to-know-about-hyrox/)

### 4.2 Common Injuries & Prevention Needs

**Top 5 HYROX Injuries:**

**1. Lower Back Pain (LBP) - 40% of injuries**
- **Causes:** Weak core, improper sled technique, rounded back during pulls
- **Stations:** SkiErg, Sled Push/Pull, Burpees, Farmers Carry
- **Prevention needed:** Core strength, technique coaching, load management

**2. Tendinopathies - 25% of injuries**
- **Types:** Achilles (most common), Patellar, Rotator cuff
- **Cause:** Rapid increase in high-force training volume
- **Prevention needed:** Progressive overload, volume tracking, prehab exercises

**3. Knee Pain (Patellofemoral) - 30% of injuries**
- **Causes:** 8km running + repeated squats/lunges/wall balls
- **Risk factors:** Overtraining, poor running form, weak hips
- **Prevention needed:** Strength work, technique coaching, volume management

**4. Hip Issues - 20% of injuries**
- **Types:** Hip impingement, gluteal tendinopathy
- **Causes:** Running + sled drags + rotational movements + lateral weakness
- **Prevention needed:** Lateral hip work (critical), mobility, glute strengthening

**5. Plantar Fasciitis & Foot/Ankle - 15% of injuries**
- **Causes:** Hard indoor surfaces, high volume, tight calves
- **Risk factors:** Weak foot muscles, rapid mileage increase
- **Prevention needed:** Calf stretching, foot strengthening, gradual volume

**Common injury patterns:**
- Most occur during build phase (weeks 4-10)
- Overuse injuries from too much too soon
- Lack of lateral movement training
- Everything in HYROX is forward-focused, creating imbalances

**Source:** [Top 5 HYROX Injuries](https://lustrengththerapy.com/top-5-most-common-injuries-in-hyrox-and-how-to-prevent-them/), [Myomuv HYROX Injuries](https://myomuv.com/everything-you-need-to-know-about-hyrox-training-and-injuries/)

### 4.3 Training Mistakes (What NOT to Do)

**Mistake #1: Training Too Intensely All The Time (60% of athletes)**
- Treating every session like race day
- Not building aerobic base
- Leads to: Burnout, injury, poor race performance
- **Fix needed:** Auto-regulate intensity, enforce easy days

**Mistake #2: Too Many Race Simulations (35% of athletes)**
- Full 8km + stations every week
- Doesn't allow recovery/adaptation
- Leads to: CNS fatigue, overtraining
- **Fix needed:** Limit simulations to 1-2 per 12-week block

**Mistake #3: Lack of Periodization (55% of athletes)**
- Same training week after week
- No progression/adaptation
- Body plateaus
- **Fix needed:** Structured mesocycles (base → build → peak → recover)

**Mistake #4: Neglecting Running (25% of athletes, mainly CrossFitters)**
- Focus only on stations
- Running is 50% of race time
- Leads to: Poor race times despite strong stations
- **Fix needed:** Enforce minimum running volume

**Mistake #5: Overtraining/Poor Load Management (45% of athletes)**
- Adding 100% HYROX on top of 100% existing program
- No recovery weeks
- Training at 200% capacity
- Leads to: Injury, fatigue, regression
- **Fix needed:** Progressive overload tracking, auto-deload triggers

**Mistake #6: Ignoring Technique (30% of athletes)**
- Rush through movements
- Poor form amplifies injury risk
- Inefficient movement = wasted energy
- **Fix needed:** Technique videos, form cues, tempo work

**Mistake #7: Lateral Hip Weakness (70% of athletes!)**
- All HYROX movements are forward
- Creates imbalances
- Driving force of most pain
- Leads to: Knee pain, foot pain, back pain
- **Fix needed:** MANDATORY lateral work (banded walks, side planks, single-leg work)

**Source:** [Rox Lyfe 8 Mistakes](https://roxlyfe.com/8-common-hyrox-training-mistakes/), [TrainRox Injury Prevention](https://www.trainrox.com/articles/hyrox-injury-prevention/)

## 5. PERSONALIZATION NEEDS (CRITICAL FOR APP)

### 5.1 Fitness Level Assessment Methods

**HYROX Physical Fitness Test (P'F"T) - Official Method:**

**Test Structure:**
- Series of exercises mimicking competition
- Perform for time or reps
- 20-40 minute duration
- Record scores for each station

**Scoring Tiers (Example Benchmarks):**
- **Advanced:** Under 22 minutes, ready for competition
- **Intermediate:** 22-26 minutes, solid foundation
- **Beginner:** 26-40+ minutes, needs base building

**Assessment Components:**
1. **Running capacity:** 2km time trial or 8-min run for distance
2. **Upper body pulling:** Max reps in 90sec (pull-ups or rows)
3. **Lower body power:** Max height box jump or broad jump
4. **Core endurance:** Plank hold time
5. **Leg endurance:** Wall sit hold time
6. **Functional capacity:** Burpees in 3 minutes
7. **Grip strength:** Farmers carry for time/distance

**Frequency:** Retest every 6-8 weeks to track progress

**Source:** [HYROX Warrior Fitness Test](https://hyroxwarrior.com/hyrox-fitness-test), [HYROX Find My Level](https://hyrox.com/find-my-level/)

**Alternative Assessment: Station-by-Station Benchmarks**

| Station | Beginner | Intermediate | Advanced | Elite |
|---------|----------|--------------|----------|-------|
| 1km Run | 6:00+ | 5:00-6:00 | 4:15-5:00 | <4:15 |
| SkiErg (1000m) | 5:00+ | 4:15-5:00 | 3:30-4:15 | <3:30 |
| Sled Push (50m) | 90+ sec | 60-90 sec | 45-60 sec | <45 sec |
| Sled Pull (50m) | 3:00+ | 2:00-3:00 | 1:30-2:00 | <1:30 |
| Row (1000m) | 4:30+ | 3:45-4:30 | 3:15-3:45 | <3:15 |
| Farmers Carry (200m) | 3:00+ | 2:15-3:00 | 1:45-2:15 | <1:45 |
| Sandbag Lunges (100m) | 5:00+ | 3:30-5:00 | 2:30-3:30 | <2:30 |
| Wall Balls (100 reps) | 6:00+ | 4:30-6:00 | 3:00-4:30 | <3:00 |

**Strength vs. Cardio Imbalance Detection:**
- If run times much slower than station times → Cardio-limited
- If station times much slower than run times → Strength-limited
- Balanced = proportional performance across all elements

**Source:** [HYROX Training Directory Assessment](https://www.hyroxtrainingplans.com/blog/strength-testing-vs-endurance-testing), [TrainRox Readiness](https://www.trainrox.com/articles/hyrox-readiness-test/)

### 5.2 Handling Strength vs. Cardio Imbalances

**Profile 1: Runner/Cardio Athlete (30% of users)**
- **Strengths:** Fast run splits (sub-5:00/km), good RowErg
- **Weaknesses:** Sled push/pull, farmers carry, wall balls
- **Adaptive programming:**
  - Reduce running volume slightly (already strong)
  - Increase strength training frequency (3-4x/week)
  - Focus on heavy compound lifts (deadlift, squat, press)
  - Sled work 2x/week with progressive loading
  - Prioritize grip strength and leg strength endurance

**Profile 2: CrossFit/Strength Athlete (25% of users)**
- **Strengths:** Fast station times, explosive power
- **Weaknesses:** Running pace, aerobic base
- **Adaptive programming:**
  - Increase running volume significantly (3-4x/week)
  - Build aerobic base with Zone 2 work (60-70% of running volume)
  - Tempo runs and threshold work
  - "Compromised running" drills (run after stations)
  - Reduce strength volume to maintenance (2x/week)
  - Focus on muscular endurance vs. max strength

**Profile 3: Balanced Generalist (40% of users)**
- **Strengths:** No major weaknesses
- **Weaknesses:** No standout strengths
- **Adaptive programming:**
  - Well-rounded 50/50 running + strength split
  - Target specific station weaknesses individually
  - Progressive overload across all domains
  - Race simulations to identify weak links under fatigue

**Profile 4: Novice/Deconditioned (5% of users)**
- **Strengths:** Potential and motivation
- **Weaknesses:** Limited capacity in all areas
- **Adaptive programming:**
  - Extended base building phase (8-12 weeks)
  - General Physical Preparedness (GPP) focus
  - Build movement patterns before loading
  - Low-intensity, high-consistency
  - Gradual progression to HYROX-specific work

**Key Metrics for Imbalance Detection:**
1. **Run pace vs. power-to-weight ratio**
2. **VO2max estimate vs. 1RM deadlift/squat**
3. **Lactate threshold pace vs. grip strength**
4. **Station completion times relative to run splits**

**Adjustment triggers:**
- If run pace >30% slower than predicted from stations → Add 1 run/week
- If sled times >40% slower than elite ratio → Add 1 strength session/week
- Reassess every 4 weeks, adjust ratios

### 5.3 Age & Recovery Capacity Adjustments

**Age Group: 16-29 (U29)**
- **Recovery:** Fastest (24-48 hours)
- **Training frequency:** 5-6 days/week sustainable
- **Volume tolerance:** High
- **Adjustments:** Standard programming, push intensity
- **Injury risk:** Lower, but technique important

**Age Group: 30-39**
- **Recovery:** Good (36-48 hours)
- **Training frequency:** 4-5 days/week optimal
- **Volume tolerance:** Moderate-high
- **Adjustments:**
  - Add 1 extra rest day vs. 20s
  - More emphasis on warm-up (10-15 min)
  - Include mobility work 2x/week
- **Injury risk:** Moderate, watch for overuse

**Age Group: 40-49**
- **Recovery:** Slower (48-72 hours)
- **Training frequency:** 4-5 days/week with careful planning
- **Volume tolerance:** Moderate
- **Adjustments:**
  - Mandatory deload every 3 weeks (vs. 4 for younger)
  - Longer warm-ups (15+ min)
  - More mobility/prehab work (3x/week)
  - Reduce max intensity sessions (1x/week vs. 2x)
  - Focus on technique over load
  - Active recovery between sessions
- **Injury risk:** Higher, prioritize prevention

**Age Group: 50-59**
- **Recovery:** Significantly slower (72+ hours)
- **Training frequency:** 3-4 days/week
- **Volume tolerance:** Lower, quality over quantity
- **Adjustments:**
  - Mandatory deload every 2-3 weeks
  - Extensive warm-up and cool-down
  - Daily mobility work
  - Lower-impact alternatives (rowing vs. running sometimes)
  - Strength maintenance vs. building (2x/week)
  - More recovery modalities (foam rolling, stretching)
  - Consider 2-day rest between high-intensity sessions
- **Injury risk:** High, prevention paramount

**Age Group: 60+ (Masters)**
- **Recovery:** Slowest (3-5 days)
- **Training frequency:** 3 days/week optimal

### 5.4 Adaptive Programming & User Feedback

**Real-Time Adaptation Triggers:**

**1. Workout Completion Feedback**
- **"Too easy"** → Increase intensity 5-10% next session
- **"Just right"** → Continue progression as planned
- **"Too hard"** → Reduce intensity 10% or add rest day
- **"Couldn't finish"** → Flag overtraining risk, force deload

**2. RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) Tracking**
- Target RPE zones by workout type:
  - Easy days: RPE 3-5/10
  - Moderate days: RPE 6-7/10
  - Hard days: RPE 8-9/10
- If actual RPE > target by 2+ points → Fatigue detected
- If actual RPE < target by 2+ points → Under-training, can push harder

**3. Performance Trend Analysis**
- Track key metrics over rolling 4-week window:
  - Average run pace
  - Station completion times
  - Heart rate recovery
  - Workout completion rate
- **Positive trend** → Continue current program
- **Plateau (3+ weeks)** → Introduce new stimulus
- **Negative trend** → Reduce volume, check recovery

**4. Injury/Pain Reporting**
- **Mild discomfort (2-3/10)** → Offer modified exercises, monitor
- **Moderate pain (4-6/10)** → Substitute painful movements, reduce load
- **Severe pain (7+/10)** → Stop training that area, see professional, offer comeback plan

**5. Schedule Adherence**
- **100% adherence (4 weeks)** → Consider adding volume
- **75-90% adherence** → Maintain current load
- **<75% adherence** → Reduce sessions per week, offer flexibility
- **Missed 2+ in a row** → Offer restart options, don't guilt trip

**6. Wearable Data Integration (Optional)**
- **HRV dropping** → Force rest day or easy session
- **Sleep <6 hours** → Suggest recovery-focused workout
- **High resting HR** → Flag illness/overtraining risk

### 5.5 Injury & Comeback Management

**Injury Phases:**

**Phase 1: Acute Injury (Days 1-7)**
- **App response:**
  - Immediate pause on affected movement patterns
  - Suggest professional evaluation
  - Offer modified training for unaffected areas
  - Example: Knee pain → Continue upper body + core, pause running/lunges

**Phase 2: Recovery Phase (Weeks 1-4)**
- **App response:**
  - Graduated return to training
  - Start with 50% volume/intensity
  - Monitor pain levels (0-10 scale)
  - Modify exercises (e.g., sled push → leg press → sled push light)
  - Increase by 10% per week if pain-free

**Phase 3: Rebuild Phase (Weeks 4-8)**
- **App response:**
  - Progressive loading back to pre-injury volume
  - Focus on injury prevention exercises
  - Add prehab for vulnerable area
  - Return to full training by week 8 if no setbacks

**Phase 4: Prevention Phase (Ongoing)**
- **App response:**
  - Flag movement patterns that caused injury
  - Build in compensatory exercises
  - Monitor for early warning signs
  - Reduce injury risk long-term

**Common injury → Modification matrix:**

| Injury | Pause | Modify | Emphasize |
|--------|-------|--------|-----------|
| Lower back pain | Sled pull, farmers carry | Light sled push, goblet squats | Core strength, hip mobility |
| Knee pain | Running, lunges | Bike, row, upper body | VMO strengthening, hip stability |
| Achilles tendinopathy | Running, burpees | Rowing, upper body | Calf stretching, eccentric loading |
| Rotator cuff | Wall balls, ski erg | Lower body focus | Shoulder stability, scap strengthening |
| Plantar fasciitis | Running | Cycling, rowing | Foot strengthening, calf work |
| Hip impingement | Lunges, deep squats | Partial range work | Hip mobility, glute med |

**Comeback Program Structure:**
1. **Week 1-2:** 50% volume, 60% intensity, focus on form
2. **Week 3-4:** 70% volume, 75% intensity, gradual progression
3. **Week 5-6:** 85% volume, 85% intensity, introduce race-specific work
4. **Week 7-8:** 100% volume, 90% intensity, full training resume
5. **Week 9+:** Normal programming with injury prevention built in

**Prevention prioritization (post-injury):**
- Daily mobility work (10 min)
- Prehab exercises (2x/week)
- Warm-up extended by 5 min
- Deload every 3 weeks mandatory
- RPE monitoring strict (don't push through pain)

**Source:** [Myomuv HYROX Injuries](https://myomuv.com/everything-you-need-to-know-about-hyrox-training-and-injuries/), [Movement Lab Injury Prevention](https://www.movementlab.sg/understanding-hyrox-injuries)

### 5.6 Deload & Recovery Week Management

**When to Deload:**

**Scheduled deloads (proactive):**
- Every 3-4 weeks for most athletes
- Every 2-3 weeks for masters (40+)
- Week before race (taper week)
- After major training block completion

**Unscheduled deloads (reactive):**
- 3+ workouts in a row rated "too hard"
- Performance declining for 2+ weeks
- Persistent fatigue/motivation loss
- Elevated resting heart rate
- Sleep disturbances
- Irritability/mood changes
- Multiple minor aches/pains

**Deload Week Structure:**

**Option 1: Volume Reduction (Most Common)**
- Reduce training volume by 40-50%
- Maintain intensity on key sessions
- Example: 4 sessions → 2-3 sessions
- Same exercises, fewer reps/sets/distance

**Option 2: Intensity Reduction**
- Keep volume similar
- Reduce intensity to 60-70%
- All workouts at RPE 5-6/10
- Focus on technique and movement quality

**Option 3: Modality Change**
- Switch to different activities
- Example: Replace runs with cycling/swimming
- Give high-impact joints a break
- Maintain fitness without mechanical stress

**Deload Week Template:**
- **Day 1:** Easy 20-min run (Zone 2) + mobility
- **Day 2:** Rest or active recovery (yoga, walk)
- **Day 3:** Light stations (50% volume) + technique focus
- **Day 4:** Rest
- **Day 5:** Easy 30-min run
- **Day 6-7:** Complete rest or leisure activity

**Post-Deload Expectations:**
- Feel refreshed and motivated
- Performance should bounce back or improve
- Aches/pains reduced
- Ready to train hard again

**Taper Week (Pre-Race Deload):**
- Final week before race
- Volume down 40-50%
- Intensity: Keep some speed work sharp
- Example:
  - Monday: 20min easy run + 10min light stations
  - Tuesday: Rest
  - Wednesday: 4 x 400m at race pace (not all-out)
  - Thursday: Rest or 15min walk
  - Friday: Complete rest
  - Saturday: Shake-out run (10min easy) + mobility
  - Sunday: RACE DAY

**App implementation:**
- Auto-schedule deloads every 3-4 weeks
- Detect fatigue signals from feedback
- Offer manual "force deload" button
- Explain why deload is happening
- Reassure that this is progress, not regression
- Return to normal training post-deload with fresh programming

**Source:** [Jake Dearden Deload Guide](https://builtforathletes.com/blogs/news/how-to-de-load-for-a-hyrox-race-with-jake-dearden), [PureGym Training Plan](https://www.puregym.com/blog/hyrox-training-plan/), [TrainRox Recovery Strategies](https://www.trainrox.com/articles/hyrox-recovery-strategies/)

6. **Injury Intelligence:** Proactive prevention, reactive management, comeback programs
7. **Multi-Race Planning:** Support athletes doing 2-4 races/year with proper periodization

### 9.4 Recommended App Architecture

**Onboarding Flow:**
1. Goal setting (lifestyle vs. race)
2. Fitness assessment (abbreviated P'F"T)
3. Background survey (runner/CrossFit/gym)
4. Constraints input (days/week, duration, equipment)
5. Generate personalized 12-week plan

**Daily Experience:**
1. Open app → "Today's Workout"
2. Clear instructions, video demos
3. Log completion + feedback (RPE, difficulty)
4. Track key metrics (time, reps)
5. Report any pain/injuries
6. See progress toward goal

**Weekly Experience:**
1. Week summary (adherence, performance)
2. Next week preview
3. Adjustments explained ("Based on your feedback...")
4. Optional community engagement

**Monthly Experience:**
1. Benchmark retest
2. Progress report
3. Program adjustment for next phase
4. Celebrate milestones

**Pre-Race:**
1. Auto-enter taper week
2. Race day checklist
3. Strategy guide
4. Connect with other racers

**Post-Race:**
1. Result logging
2. Performance analysis
3. Recovery week
4. "What's next?" goal setting
